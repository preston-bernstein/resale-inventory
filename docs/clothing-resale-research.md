## 1. Prep: Cleaning, Repair, and What NOT to Sell

### Cleaning standards before listing
- **Machine wash** sturdy fabrics (cotton, denim). **Hand wash** delicates and vintage items. Wash sweaters cold, gentle cycle, to preserve fabric integrity. (2ulaundry.com, jessoshii.com)
- **Pre-soak** in water + detergent for 15–30 minutes before washing to lift deep-set odor/grime, especially on thrifted items. (jessoshii.com)
- **Pre-treat stains before washing**, not after — washing first can set a stain permanently. (jessoshii.com)
- **Stain removal recipe**: mix equal parts 3% hydrogen peroxide and blue Dawn dish soap, apply directly, work in gently with fingers/soft brush, let sit 30–60 min before laundering. Alternative: oxygen-based bleach or enzyme stain removers (safe on most fabric, won't damage quality). (underpriced.app)
- **Odor removal**: if a thrifted item has a strong smell, don't wash immediately — hang outside a few hours first to air out. Add white vinegar to the rinse cycle as a natural disinfectant/odor-reducer. Activated charcoal bags in storage bins prevent odor transfer between inventory items. (jessoshii.com, 3niterscorner.com)
- **Pilling removal**: use a handheld fabric shaver ($10–15 on Amazon) on sweaters/coats — near-instant fix, transforms perceived condition/value. (underpriced.app)
- **Steaming after washing** kills germs and freshens fabric, especially useful for items that can't be tumble-dried. (jessoshii.com)
- Bottom line from underpriced.app: cleaning + minor restoration (fabric shaving, steaming, button/zipper fixes) can add **$20–50 in perceived value for about $5 in supplies**.

### Ironing vs. steaming (thelaundress.com, rd.com, gentlemansgazette.com)
- **Iron**: best for cotton, linen, structured garments (dress shirts, blouses, work slacks) needing a crisp, pressed finish. Direct heat/pressure handles stubborn wrinkles on heavy fabric.
- **Steamer**: best for delicates — silk, chiffon, synthetics, corduroy, velvet — anything that could scorch or crush under an iron. Steaming is also simply faster, useful for batch-prepping many items before a photo session.
- Neither is universally "better" — match tool to fabric.

### What NOT to sell
- **Recalled products**: illegal to sell a recalled item; resellers are legally expected to check. Search cpsc.gov/recalls before listing anything you're unsure about, especially kids' items and electricals. Selling a recalled item can expose you to liability if it causes injury. (CPSC Resellers Guide, cpsc.gov)
- **Counterfeits**: selling counterfeit goods that infringe a registered trademark is a federal crime — up to $2M fine and 10 years in prison for a first offense (trafficking scale, but the legal exposure exists at any scale). Don't list unauthenticated "inspired by" or clearly fake designer goods as genuine. (cpsc.gov / legalclarity.org)
- **Damaged/unsafe items generally**: don't sell anything broken, structurally compromised, or missing safety-relevant parts (e.g., drawstrings on kids' hoodies — CPSC has documented 26 deaths/73 non-fatal incidents 1985–2019 tied to hood/neck drawstrings snagging). Damaged goods sold without disclosure can create seller liability. (cpsc.gov)
- **Practical "too worn" bar** (synthesized from reseller-education consensus across sources above): visible holes (non-designed), underarm yellowing/staining that didn't lift after treatment, broken zippers/elastic you haven't fixed, strong odor that survived a wash — these should go to textile recycling/donation, not resale. Undisclosed flaws drive returns and bad ratings, which compounds against you on every platform's ranking algorithm.

## 2. Photography

### Lighting
- **Natural light** near a window during the day is the default recommendation — soft and even. Avoid direct sunlight (harsh shadows, color distortion). North- or east-facing windows give the most diffuse, even light; south/west windows run stronger and shadow-prone. (multiple sources via ocus.com, pixelz.com)
- **Softbox** is the "industry gold standard" if going artificial — diffuses harsh light across a wider area, minimizing shadow/glare. (pixelz.com)
- **Lightbox sizing**: pick a box 4–6" larger than your biggest item in each dimension. Large lightboxes (35"+) needed for flat-lay clothing; most sellers start with a 20–24" box as an all-purpose middle ground (fine for shoes/accessories, too small for full garments). (nightjar.so)

### Background
- Flat lays: plain white or gray background, no texture/pattern competing with the garment. White poster board or foam board works as a cheap backdrop. (techcloudltd.com)

### Shot list per listing (synthesized consensus, esp. crosslisting guidance from listperfectly.com)
Recommended order/coverage for a full listing, applicable across platforms:
1. Hero/front shot (on-body or flat lay — see below)
2. Back of garment
3. Brand/size tag close-up
4. Fabric content tag close-up (buyers search by material)
5. Any flaw, stain, hole, repair — photographed close and honestly, in normal light (not hidden or cropped out)
6. Measurement shot: tape measure laid across the garment on a flat surface, clearly readable (builds buyer trust, reduces returns/disputes)
7. Detail shots: buttons, zippers, embroidery, hardware, logo close-ups if distinguishing

### Flat lay vs. on-body vs. mannequin/ghost mannequin
- **On-model/on-body photos convert 20–30% higher** than flat lay across most clothing categories in e-commerce data, and time-on-listing increases 40–60%. On-model sets realistic fit expectations, reducing fit-related returns (42% of fashion returns are attributed to size/fit issues generally). (metamodels.ai)
- **Flat lay weakness**: can't convey drape, structure, or true fit — buyers can't tell how a dress cinches or how a blazer's shoulders sit. Higher return/lower conversion than on-model. (photta.app)
- **Ghost mannequin**: middle ground — better structure visualization than flat lay, cheaper than a model shoot. Good compromise if you don't want to model items yourself.
- **Practical takeaway for a solo first-time seller**: wearing the item yourself (or a consistent mannequin) for at least the hero shot measurably helps; use flat lay for the supporting/detail shots (tags, flaws, measurements).

### Phone camera settings (prizminstitute.com, makeuseof.com, tomsguide.com)
- **Turn off flash** — built-in phone flash creates harsh light, blown highlights, color distortion. Use natural/window light or a lamp instead.
- **HDR**: useful in high-contrast scenes (bright window light + shadowed garment) but can "ghost" on any movement and over-process — use selectively, not as a blanket default.
- **Enable grid lines** (rule-of-thirds overlay) to keep garments centered/level — small thing, consistently recommended.
- Use a **higher aperture equivalent / focus lock** (tap to focus on the fabric, not the background) for sharp detail across the garment surface.
- Keep **white balance** consistent/accurate to the lighting so colors read true — a common complaint from buyers is "item was a different color than pictured," which drives returns.

### Photo count limits by platform (2026)
- **Poshmark**: up to 16 photos + 1 video per listing. (docs.nifty.ai)
- **eBay**: up to 24 photos per listing. (industry sources)
- **Depop**: up to 4 photos per listing (much tighter — pick your best shots: hero, back/detail, tag, flaw). (closo.co)
- **Mercari**: no hard number surfaced in research, but practice mirrors Poshmark (multiple angles + tag + flaws).
- Crosslisting guidance when going from a platform with more slots to Depop's 4: prioritize hero shot → brand/tag → flat lay → flaw, drop the rest.

### Common mistakes (underpriced.app, and cross-cutting through photography sources)
- Dark, blurry, or cluttered photos — directly correlated with lower sale price and slower sell-through (one source cites better photos → 73% higher sale price, 4x faster sales as a rule of thumb from reseller education content — treat as directional, not verified statistic).
- Photographing on a messy/patterned background (bed, carpet) that distracts from the item.
- Wrinkled or pet-hair-covered garments in photos — steam/lint-roll before shooting, not after.
- Inconsistent lighting/background across a batch, which makes a seller's storefront look unprofessional (batch-shoot 10–20 items at once under the same setup to keep consistency and save time — voolist.com workflow guidance).

## 3. Platform Selection by Item Type/Value, Fees, and Payout Timing (2026)

### Quick-match guide (synthesized from sellygenie.com, fashiongoggled.com, voolist.com, closo.co)
| Item profile | Best platform(s) | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Designer/luxury (Chanel, LV, Hermès, Gucci) | The RealReal, Vestiaire Collective, Fashionphile | Authentication service buyers trust; higher-dollar audience |
| Streetwear/menswear (Supreme, Rick Owens, Kith, vintage band tees) | Grailed | Niche, knowledgeable buyer base that pays for authenticity/rarity |
| Everyday brands, women's fashion, activewear (Lululemon, J.Crew) | Poshmark | Large fashion-focused, social-selling audience willing to pay more than Vinted/Mercari |
| Fast fashion / high volume / lower price point | Vinted | Zero seller fees maximize take-home on low-ticket items |
| Beginner-friendly, simple interface, mixed inventory | Mercari, Vinted | No social-selling learning curve, straightforward listing flow |
| Rare/collectible, auction-suited, or item needs price discovery | eBay | Auction format + huge search volume, best "sold comps" data of any platform |
| Bulk closet clean-out, minimal effort, willing to accept low payout for convenience | ThredUp | Mail-in, they list/sell/ship — you do almost nothing |

### Fee structures (as of 2026)

**Poshmark** (support.poshmark.com, voolist.com)
- Sales **under $15**: flat **$2.95** commission, seller keeps the rest.
- Sales **$15+**: seller keeps **80%**, Poshmark takes **20%**.
- No listing, monthly, or payment-processing fees on top of that.
- **Shipping**: Poshmark provides a prepaid USPS Priority label; buyer pays a flat **$7.67** for standard shipping up to 5 lbs. Seller pays nothing extra for in-weight packages.
- **Overweight packages (5–10 lbs)**: seller must "upgrade" the label in-app; cost is **~$4.99 per additional pound**, auto-deducted from seller earnings — do this before shipping if item won't fit under 5 lbs, or USPS will flag it and Poshmark deducts postage anyway. (support.poshmark.com)
- **Payout**: funds held in escrow until buyer accepts delivery, or automatically after **3 days** post-delivery if no action. Withdraw via bank transfer, Venmo, PayPal, or use as in-app credit. (closo.co)

**eBay** (ebay.com, feecalculator.pro)
- **Final value fee**: ~**13.6%** of total sale price (item + shipping charged to buyer) for most clothing categories as the general model; some clothing subcategories were raised to **15.3%** in 2026 — check the specific category before assuming 13.6%.
- Plus a **per-order fee**: **$0.30** for orders ≤$10, **$0.40** for orders over $10.
- Fee is calculated on the **total transaction amount including shipping** — not just item price.
- **Payout**: funds available for withdrawal within **~2 days** after buyer payment confirmation, subject to a hold if there's an open case/dispute.

**Depop** (depophelp.zendesk.com, sellerfeecalc.com)
- **$0 Depop selling/commission fee** for US and UK sellers as of 2026 (a change from the old 10% model).
- Instead: **payment processing fee of 3.3% + $0.45** per transaction.
- Example: $50 sale → **$2.10** total in fees, seller nets $47.90 before cost of goods/shipping/promotion.
- Shipping fee charged to buyer is passed to seller in the payout — Depop does **not** take a cut of the shipping charge.
- **Payout**: Depop Payments deposits to bank **1–2 days** after sale, or up to **10 days** for new sellers (fraud-hold period).
- **Prepaid label pricing tiers** (USPS Ground Advantage, generated in-app): Extra Small (<4oz) ~$4.49, Small (<8oz) ~$5.39, Medium (<1lb) ~$7.99, Large (<2lb) ~$12.25, Extra Large (<10lb) ~$16.15.
- Australia: Depop is dropping seller fees entirely from **22 July 2026** (processing fees still apply) — not relevant to US sellers but shows the direction of travel.

**Mercari** (mercari.com/help_center, underpriced.app)
- **10% flat seller fee** on item price **plus** buyer-paid shipping, effective for listings created/updated after **Jan 6, 2026**. This replaced the old model that also charged a 2.9% + $0.50 payment-processing fee — that separate processing fee is now gone (folded into the 10%).
- Example: $50 sale + $5 shipping → seller fee is **$5.50** total (10% of $55).
- **Payout fees**: **$2.00** for standard direct deposit, **$3.00** for Instant Pay.
- **Prepaid label pricing**: **$4.99** (up to 4oz) to **$15.50** (up to 5 lbs). Package must be ≤100 lbs or longest side ≤30"/girth ≤130" to ship on Mercari's label; heavier needs self-arranged freight (flat $2,500 freight rate over 150 lbs — irrelevant for clothing but shows the ceiling).
- **Payout timing**: buyer has 72 hours post-delivery to rate the order; funds release to Mercari balance on rating (or automatically after the window). Balance can be spent in-app or direct-deposited to a bank account.

**Vinted** (vinted.com/pricelist, voolist.com)
- **Zero seller fees** — no listing fee, no commission, no final value fee, no payment processing fee. Sell a $25 item, keep $25.
- Cost is shifted to the **buyer**: a "Buyer Protection fee" of roughly **5% of item price + $0.70**, charged at checkout, covers payment processing/buyer protection/support.
- Shipping paid by buyer, prepaid label generated by Vinted post-sale.
- Optional paid seller promotion ("Bumps") to boost visibility — the only seller-side cost, and it's opt-in.
- This makes Vinted the best net-take-home platform for low-to-mid ticket, high-volume fast-fashion items specifically.

**ThredUp** (thredup.com, underpriced.app, help.thredup.com)
- Mail-in consignment model: order a free "Clean Out Kit" polybag, fill it, ship free via prepaid label, ThredUp evaluates over **2–8 weeks**.
- **Acceptance rate**: ~60–80% of items in Standard Clean Outs, 80–90% in Premium Clean Outs get accepted; rejected items are donated by default (or returned to you for a **$10.99** fee).
- **Payout**: you only get paid once an accepted item actually **sells** on ThredUp's site — not on drop-off. Payout is **5–80% of resale price depending on brand/value tier**; typical realized payout for low-value items ($5–15 listing range) is just **5–15%** of sale price (i.e., $2.49–$3.87 average per accepted item in that tier).
- Best use case: bulk, low-effort clean-out of mid-tier/lower-value everyday clothing where your time is worth more than maximizing per-item dollars. Not the platform for anything you want top dollar on.

**The RealReal** (therealreal.com, closo.co, investedluxury.com)
- Full-service luxury consignment, **not a self-list platform** — you ship/drop off, they authenticate, photograph, price, and sell.
- Commission ranges roughly **20–80%** of sale price depending on category/value (i.e., you as consignor get anywhere from ~20% to ~80%, inverted framing depends on source — treat commission-to-you as **40–50%** typical for a first-time consignor, higher tiers reward high-volume/qualifying consignors, e.g. 50% on items selling $100–$199.99).
- Best for genuinely high-end designer pieces where authentication and a luxury-buyer audience matter more than maximizing your %.

**Vestiaire Collective** (faq.vestiairecollective.com, underpriced.app)
- Self-list with authentication service. **Tiered seller commission by item value**: items <$100 → 25%; $100–$499 → 20%; $500–$1,999 → 18%; $2,000–$4,999 → 15%; $5,000+ → 12%.
- Plus payment processing: **3% (min $3)**.
- Listing is free; first several listings may be fee-free as a new-seller incentive.
- Example comparison sourced: on a $2,500 bag, Vestiaire nets the seller ~$2,125 vs. The RealReal's ~$1,500 — Vestiaire is meaningfully better economics for higher-value single items if you're comfortable self-listing/authenticating vs. RealReal's white-glove service.

**Grailed** (grailed.com, voolist.com, closo.co)
- No listing/monthly/promoted-listing fees — pay only on sale.
- **9% commission + payment processing of 3.49% + $0.49**, effective total **~12.5% + $0.49** per sale.
- Payout goes to PayPal directly.
- Offers on Grailed are **binding** once accepted by the seller (different from Poshmark/Mercari's more casual offer culture) — sets buyer/seller expectations that a deal is a deal.
- Best for streetwear (Supreme, Palace, Stussy, BAPE, Kith, Fear of God), designer menswear (Rick Owens, Raf Simons, Margiela), Japanese labels (Kapital, Visvim, Sacai), luxury sneakers, and vintage (band tees, military surplus, workwear).

### Fee comparison at a glance (illustrative — see per-platform detail above for exact math)
- Lowest seller-side fee: **Vinted (0%)** > **Depop (0% commission + ~3.3%+$0.45 processing)** > **Mercari (10% flat)** > **Grailed (~12.5%+$0.49)** > **eBay (~13.6–15.3%+per-order fee)** > **Poshmark (20% over $15, flat $2.95 under)** > **Vestiaire (12–25% tiered)+processing** > **The RealReal / ThredUp (lowest seller take-home %, but zero listing effort)**.

## 4. Pricing Strategy

### Research comps, not asking prices
- The consensus across every reseller-education source: **price using SOLD listings, never active/asking listings.** Active listings show what people hope to get; sold listings show what buyers actually paid. eBay's sold-listing filter is repeatedly called the "gold standard" for comps research because of its search volume and history, even if you're pricing to sell on a different platform. (gavelbase.com, closo.co, voolist.com)
- Process: search the exact brand + item name/style + size on eBay (and Poshmark's own sold-filter search) → filter to "sold" → look at the distribution of realized prices, not just one data point → adjust for your item's condition relative to the comps you found.

### Condition is the single biggest price lever
- A shirt with a small stain can sell for **~40% less** than an otherwise-identical pristine piece — condition dominates brand/style in determining realized price within a comp set. (underpriced.app)
- Other price drivers, in rough order after condition: brand recognition/demand, size (mid-range sizes sell faster/higher than very small or very large), seasonality (list winter coats in fall, not July), trending style tags (Y2K, cottagecore, minimalist — apply only if genuinely accurate, don't keyword-stuff).

### Two competing pricing philosophies (both cited as valid depending on goal)
1. **Price low / competitive**: works for ~90% of items per one source — most buyers are deal-seekers. Price near (not necessarily at) the lowest of the active-listing range, as long as it's still consistent with sold comps. Optimizes for **speed of sale**.
2. **Price high + negotiate down**: list ~15% above your real target price, then proactively send "offers to likers" (see Poshmark mechanic below) at a discount that lands at your true target. One 2026 source claims this yields the highest *conversion* rate on Poshmark specifically, because it lets you use the platform's offer/discount mechanics as the closing tool rather than the sticker price. Optimizes for **perceived value / anchoring**.
- Practical read for a beginner: use low/competitive pricing on **fast fashion / high-volume / Vinted-Mercari-style items** where speed matters more than a few dollars; use the high-anchor + offer approach on **Poshmark specifically** and on items with real brand cachet, where the offer mechanic exists and buyers expect to negotiate.

### Bundle and offer mechanics (Poshmark-specific, but the psychology generalizes)
- **Bundle discount**: set a store-wide automatic discount (e.g., "15% off 2+ items") in seller settings — encourages buyers to add a second item rather than pay full shipping twice. (support.poshmark.com)
- **Offers to Likers**: any user who "likes" a listing can be sent a private, time-limited offer. Mechanic requires the seller to drop price by **at least 10%** and pick a shipping discount; offer **expires in 24 hours**. This is Poshmark's core re-engagement tool for stale listings. (support.poshmark.com, posherva.com)
- **Timing cadence**: experienced sellers commonly send offers to likers **twice a month, around the 1st and 16th** — timed to align with common paycheck dates. (recycledrosesguide.com)
- **Closet Clear Out events**: Poshmark-run promotional periods where you drop a price ≥10% and Poshmark (not you) subsidizes the shipping discount — a low-cost way to move stale inventory. (blog.poshmark.com)

### Markdown cadence / floor price discipline
- General guidance: **if an item hasn't sold in ~2 weeks, cut price 10–15%** and re-evaluate. Re-listing (delete + relist, or "share" to bump position) resets visibility in feed-based algorithms and can trigger a fresh wave of likes to re-target with offers. (voolist.com)
- Set a **floor price before you list** — the minimum you'll accept factoring in platform fee %, shipping cost you'll eat if any, and what you paid (if bought as inventory) or opportunity cost (if from your own closet). Never negotiate below the floor live in the moment; decide it in advance.
- Don't decline lowball offers outright — **counter them**. Reseller-education consensus: most successful sales on offer-based platforms start with a lowball that gets countered to a middle price, not a walk-away. (underpriced.app)

## 5. Listing Copy: Titles, Descriptions, SEO

### Title formula (converges across Poshmark-specific and general marketplace SEO sources)
**Brand + Item Type + Distinguishing Model/Style + Key Attribute (color/material) + Size**

Examples cited directly from sources:
- "Lululemon Align High Rise Leggings Black Size 6"
- "Coach Abbie Crossbody Bag 9017 in White"

Rationale for the ordering (quicklistai.org, posherva.com, closetassistantpm.com):
1. **Brand first** — brand is the single most-searched term in marketplace search; buyers filter by brand before anything else.
2. **Item type / model** next — second most-filtered field (buyers searching "leggings" or a specific product line/model number).
3. **Size, color, condition** follow — these are hard filters buyers apply to narrow results.
4. **Style/use-case keywords last** — captures long-tail search ("yoga," "vintage," "oversized," "Y2K") without cluttering the primary identifying info.

### Description structure
- Lead with the **most important facts first**: brand, size, condition, key measurements — buyers skim, so front-load.
- **One detail per line** — improves skimmability versus a dense paragraph. (hustleandslow.com)
- Repeat main keywords naturally (once or twice, not stuffed) and include reasonable synonyms/alternate terms a buyer might search.
- Always include **condition, measurements, and care instructions** in the body even if covered elsewhere in the listing fields.
- **Avoid keyword stuffing** — Poshmark's algorithm can specifically detect and penalize (lower ranking for) irrelevant trending-term stuffing (e.g., tagging "Y2K" on an item with no relation). Same logic likely applies to other platforms' search-ranking systems even where not explicitly documented. (posherva.com)

### Measurement conventions — take flat, not on-body
The **flat-lay measurement method** is the accepted cross-platform standard: lay the garment flat, smooth it out, measure straight across specific points, double where the source explains ("doubling" accounts for measuring only one side of a symmetric garment). (thetailoredco.com, as-good-as-ever.com)

- **Tops/jackets — pit-to-pit (chest)**: measure straight across from armpit seam to armpit seam; **double** for full chest/bust circumference.
- **Pants — waist**: lay flat, buttoned/zipped, measure straight across the waistband; **double** for full waist circumference.
- **Pants — inseam**: turn inside out, measure from the crotch seam down the inner leg to the cuff (no doubling — this is already a single-side length).
- **Pants — rise**: measure from crotch seam to top of waistband, front.
- **Dresses — bust**: same pit-to-pit method as tops, doubled.
- **Dresses — waist**: measure at the narrowest point, flat, doubled.
- Use a **flexible cloth/vinyl tape measure** — the standard tool for accuracy on curved seams (armholes, waistlines).
- **Include a photo of the tape measure laid across the garment** as part of your shot list — this single practice is repeatedly cited as a trust-builder that reduces size-related disputes/returns, since brand size tags are notoriously inconsistent across labels and eras.

## 6. Shipping

### Weighing and package prep
- Use a **kitchen scale** as a basic shipping-station tool — cited universally in beginner workflow guides as a startup essential alongside tape, poly mailers, and a printer. Weigh with all packaging materials included (label rounding matters directly to per-shipment fee tiers).

### Poly mailers vs. boxes (shipbob.com, jiaropack.com, tpplasticusa.com)
- **Poly mailers**: default choice for clothing — soft goods (shirts, jeans, most apparel) aren't at risk of crush damage, so a mailer's lack of rigid protection doesn't matter. Roughly **$0.25/mailer vs. ~$1.25/box** of comparable size — meaningfully cheaper at volume. Also lighter (keeps you in a lower weight tier) and water/moisture-resistant (protects against rain/snow in transit). No assembly/tape needed, unlike boxes.
- **Boxes**: reserve for structured or embellished items — e.g., dresses with beading/sequins/delicate ornamentation that could crease or catch in a soft mailer, or when bundling multiple bulky items together.
- Practical stocking strategy: keep both on hand; default to poly mailers, reach for a box only when the item has structure/embellishment that needs protecting.

### Carrier/service choice for clothing weight bands
- **USPS Ground Advantage** is the cheapest, slowest standard USPS service (2–5 day delivery depending on zone), includes full tracking and **$100 of insurance** by default — the default choice for most clothing shipments given typical multi-day handling tolerance. (usps.com, pirateship.com)
- **Pirate Ship** (free third-party USPS/UPS label reseller, no monthly fee) gives sellers **Commercial-Plus pricing** — cited as **up to 70% savings** vs. retail USPS counter rates, with no markup/hidden fees. Relevant because Poshmark/Mercari/Depop generate their *own* prepaid labels at their own negotiated rates, but if you're shipping outside those built-in label systems (e.g., eBay, Grailed, or a Facebook Marketplace/local sale gone remote), Pirate Ship is the standard reseller-education recommendation over paying USPS retail counter price.
- **Ground Advantage Cubic rate**: priced by *dimensions only*, not weight — Pirate Ship auto rate-shops between weight-based and cubic pricing and picks whichever is cheaper. Notable for clothing specifically because garments are often **lightweight but bulky** (a coat or bundle of shirts), which is exactly the profile Cubic rates favor — can ship up to 20 lbs for no extra cost under Cubic in some cases.
- **2026 USPS retail Ground Advantage rate reference points (Zone 1, before July 12, 2026 tier change)**: up to 4oz ~$5.90 retail / ~$5.09 commercial; up to 8oz ~$7.30 retail / ~$5.58 commercial; up to 12–15.999oz ~$8.85 retail / ~$6.42 commercial; 1 lb ~$8.85 retail / ~$7.05 commercial; 2 lb ~$10.00 retail / ~$7.40 commercial; 5 lb ~$12.00 retail / ~$8.36 commercial; 10 lb ~$14.75 retail / ~$11.22 commercial. (parcelpath.com, idshipthat.app — treat as directional; rates change and a July 12, 2026 USPS rule change collapses the sub-1lb tiers into a single rate — verify current rates at time of build.)
- **July 12, 2026 USPS change**: sub-1lb Ground Advantage Commercial pricing collapses from four tiers (4oz/8oz/12oz/15.999oz) into one flat rate for anything 1oz–15.999oz. Also, the dimensional-weight divisor for packages over 1 cubic foot drops from 166 to 139, meaning **large/bulky packages will price higher** after this date. Relevant if Preston is shipping bulky bundles (e.g., a bagged bundle of multiple garments).
- **Priority Mail**: faster (1–3 day) but pricier — worth it mainly when a buyer pays for expedited shipping or for higher-value items where faster delivery + built-in $100 insurance (Ground Advantage) vs Priority's $100 also-included insurance is less the differentiator than speed itself.

### Platform-provided label mechanics
- **Poshmark**: generates a prepaid USPS Priority label automatically once a sale happens; buyer pays flat $7.67 for up to 5 lbs; seller must manually "upgrade" the label in-app before shipping if overweight (~$4.99/additional lb, 5–10 lb band), auto-deducted from earnings.
- **Mercari**: prepaid label options tiered by weight, $4.99 (≤4oz) to $15.50 (≤5 lb); must stay within the purchased label's weight/size envelope or arrange shipping yourself for anything over 100 lbs (irrelevant for nearly all clothing, but the ceiling exists).
- **Depop**: after sale, seller receives a QR code or printable label (email/app) for a Depop-negotiated USPS Ground Advantage rate, tiered by weight from ~$4.49 (XS, <4oz) to ~$16.15 (XL, <10lb); can print at nearest USPS retail location via the QR code if no home printer.
- **Vinted**: generates a prepaid label post-sale; buyer pays for it as part of checkout (separate from the Buyer Protection fee); seller prints and attaches.
- **eBay / Grailed**: no auto-generated label at sale time in the same walled-garden way — seller is expected to buy their own label (through eBay's own shipping tool, which also offers discounted rates, or externally via Pirate Ship) and upload tracking.

### Packaging and turnaround expectations
- Ship promptly — **1–2 business days** after sale is the commonly cited expectation across platforms/reseller-education content; slow handling time hurts seller ratings/rankings on every major platform.
- Keep it simple: poly mailer, tape, and printed label/QR code is sufficient for the large majority of clothing; add tissue paper or a thank-you card as an inexpensive touch that experienced sellers use to encourage repeat buyers/good ratings, though this is optional polish, not a functional requirement.
- Always ship with **tracking** (all platform-provided labels include it by default) and to the **address on file in the platform's order system**, not an address a buyer messages you directly — protects seller in case of a dispute.

## 7. Repeatable End-to-End Workflow (First-Timer, Closet → Bank Account)

Synthesized into an ordered, actionable sequence for someone starting from zero:

1. **Pull the item and assess it.** Check for stains, odor, pilling, damage, missing parts. Decide keep/sell/donate. Cross-check anything uncertain (kids' items especially) against cpsc.gov/recalls if it's an unfamiliar or off-brand product.
2. **Clean it.** Wash or hand-wash per fabric type; pre-treat any stains before washing, not after. Deodorize (air out + vinegar rinse) if needed. Run a fabric shaver over any pilling.
3. **Repair/finish.** Fix a loose button or replace nothing you can't confidently repair — flag unfixed minor flaws for disclosure instead. Iron structured pieces; steam delicates.
4. **Research comps before pricing.** Search brand + item + size on eBay/Poshmark sold listings (not active). Note the realized price range. Adjust down for any condition flaw relative to the comp set.
5. **Set your price and floor.** Price using one of the two strategies (competitive-low for fast turnover, anchor-high-plus-offers for negotiable platforms like Poshmark) — decide your walk-away floor in advance, factoring in the platform's fee % and any shipping cost you're absorbing.
6. **Choose the platform** based on item type/value (see Section 3 table): everyday brand → Poshmark/Mercari/Vinted; luxury → Vestiaire/RealReal; streetwear/menswear → Grailed; rare/auction-suited → eBay; bulk/low-effort → ThredUp.
7. **Take measurements** flat, with a visible tape-measure photo (pit-to-pit, waist, inseam/rise as applicable).
8. **Photograph** in natural window light (or lightbox), no flash, grid lines on: hero (on-body preferred), back, brand tag, fabric tag, flaws, measurement shot, relevant detail shots — respecting each platform's photo-count cap (Poshmark 16, eBay 24, Depop 4).
9. **Write the listing.** Title = Brand + Item Type + Model/Attribute + Size. Description front-loads brand/size/condition/measurements, one fact per line, natural keyword repetition, no stuffing.
10. **Publish and cross-list** (optionally via Vendoo/List Perfectly/Crosslist if selling on 2+ platforms) — set up sale-detection/auto-delist if cross-listing, so a sale on one platform doesn't leave the item live (and double-sellable) elsewhere.
11. **Engage post-listing.** Share/bump listings (Poshmark), respond to offers/lowballs with a counter rather than a decline, send "offers to likers" on a ~biweekly cadence for stale items.
12. **Reprice if stale.** No sale in ~2 weeks → cut price 10–15%, refresh listing position.
13. **On sale: ship within 1–2 business days.** Weigh accurately with packaging. Poly mailer default, box for structured/embellished items. Use the platform-provided label where available (Poshmark/Mercari/Depop/Vinted auto-generate); otherwise buy discounted postage via Pirate Ship rather than paying USPS retail counter price. Confirm package is within the purchased label's weight/size band — upgrade the label if not (Poshmark: ~$4.99/lb over 5lb).
14. **Ship to the platform's system address**, always with tracking uploaded/scanned.
15. **Wait out the payment hold.** Funds typically release 2–3 days post-delivery (Poshmark: 3 days or buyer acceptance; Mercari: buyer rates within 72 hrs or auto-release; eBay: ~2 days after payment confirmation absent a dispute).
16. **Withdraw to bank** (or reinvest as in-app credit/store credit for future purchases, if platform offers it, e.g., ThredUp Partner Kits).
17. **Repeat**, applying what sold fast/high vs. slow/low back into future pricing and platform choice.

## 8. Common Beginner Mistakes (and the Fix)

Synthesized from underpriced.app, nusource.io, hustleandslow.com, and cross-cutting through all sections above:

1. **Poor photography** (dark, blurry, cluttered background) — biggest single lever on both sale price and sell-through speed. Fix: natural light, plain background, no flash, full shot list including flaws.
2. **Pricing off active/asking listings instead of sold comps** — leads to systematically overpriced, stale listings. Fix: always filter to "sold," ideally on eBay's large sold-listing dataset even if selling elsewhere.
3. **Ignoring condition disclosure** — hiding or cropping out flaws in photos leads to returns, disputes, and rating damage that compounds against future search ranking. Fix: photograph and describe every real flaw; price accordingly instead of hiding it.
4. **Overpaying for sourced inventory** relative to realistic resale value — applies less to Preston's situation (selling from his own closet, not sourcing to flip) but still relevant to any future thrift-sourcing: research resale comps *before* buying, not after.
5. **Buying/listing too much too fast without knowing what sells** — for closet-clearing this shows up as trying to list 50 items on day one with rushed photos/pricing. Fix: batch in manageable groups, apply the same prep/photo/price discipline per item rather than rushing volume.
6. **Disorganized inventory/workspace** — lost items, missed shipping deadlines, wrong item shipped. Fix: a simple shipping station (scale, mailers, tape, labels) and a place items live between "listed" and "sold" so nothing gets buried.
7. **Slow handling time** — shipping late hurts seller metrics on every platform. Fix: commit to 1–2 business day ship time, build it into the workflow (Section 7 step 13) rather than treating shipping as an afterthought.
8. **Declining lowball offers outright instead of countering** — kills sales that could've closed at a middle price. Fix: always counter; use the pricing anchor strategy (Section 4) so there's room to counter without going below your pre-set floor.
9. **Wrong platform for the item** — e.g., listing a fast-fashion $8 top on The RealReal (wrong audience/fee structure) or a genuine designer piece on Vinted (leaves money on the table vs. an authenticated luxury platform). Fix: use the Section 3 matching table before listing.
10. **Not accounting for fees when setting price** — pricing an item at exactly your target take-home without adding back the platform's cut (20% Poshmark, 10% Mercari, etc.) means realizing less than expected. Fix: back into list price from desired net, factoring the specific platform's fee structure (Section 3).
11. **Keyword stuffing titles/descriptions** — some platforms (confirmed for Poshmark) actively penalize this in ranking. Fix: natural, front-loaded, brand-first titles; relevant tags only.
12. **Not weighing packages before buying/using a label** — leads to overweight-label penalties (Poshmark's $4.99/lb overage charge) or shipments getting held by USPS for insufficient postage. Fix: weigh with all packaging included before selecting/printing a label.
