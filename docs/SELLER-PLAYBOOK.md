# Seller Playbook: From Closet to Bank Account

A practical guide for selling clothes online, written for someone doing this for the first time. Distilled from platform help centers and reseller-education sources — see `docs/clothing-resale-research.md` for full citations and detail behind every claim here.

This guide also lives in the app at **Playbook** in the nav, in the same order, so you can check it while you're actually listing an item.

---

## The 17-step workflow

This is the whole process, start to finish. Everything below expands on one of these steps.

1. Pull the item out and assess it — stains, odor, pilling, damage, missing parts. Keep, sell, or donate?
2. Clean it (wash or hand-wash, pre-treat stains before washing).
3. Repair or finish it (fix what you can, disclose what you can't).
4. Research sold comps for pricing — never price off active listings.
5. Set your price and your walk-away floor.
6. Pick the platform that fits the item.
7. Take flat measurements with a visible tape-measure photo.
8. Photograph it (natural light, no flash, full shot list).
9. Write the listing (title formula + front-loaded description).
10. Publish it.
11. Engage — respond to offers, counter lowballs, don't ignore likes.
12. Reprice if it's been sitting 2+ weeks.
13. Ship within 1–2 business days of a sale.
14. Ship to the address on file in the platform, always with tracking.
15. Wait for the payout hold to clear.
16. Withdraw the money (or keep it as store credit).
17. Repeat, using what you learned from what sold fast vs. slow.

## 1. Prep: what to clean, fix, and skip

**Clean it properly.** Machine-wash sturdy fabrics (cotton, denim); hand-wash delicates and vintage. Pre-treat stains *before* washing — washing first can set a stain permanently. A simple stain fix: equal parts 3% hydrogen peroxide and blue Dawn dish soap, worked in gently, left 30–60 minutes before laundering.

**Deal with odor separately from stains.** Air a smelly item outside for a few hours before washing. Add white vinegar to the rinse cycle. A $10–15 fabric shaver fixes pilling on sweaters and coats almost instantly — cleaning and small fixes like this can add $20–50 in perceived value for about $5 in supplies.

**Iron vs. steam.** Iron cotton, linen, and structured pieces (dress shirts, blouses) that need a crisp finish. Steam anything that could scorch or crush under an iron — silk, chiffon, velvet, corduroy.

**What NOT to sell:**
- Anything on a CPSC recall list (check cpsc.gov/recalls if you're unsure, especially kids' items).
- Anything that could be mistaken for counterfeit designer goods — don't list unauthenticated "inspired by" pieces as genuine.
- Anything with a structural safety issue (broken drawstrings on kids' hoodies, exposed hardware, etc.).
- Anything with visible holes, staining that didn't lift, a broken zipper you haven't fixed, or odor that survived a wash. Undisclosed flaws drive returns and hurt your ratings on every platform — donate or recycle these instead.

## 2. Photography

Photos are the single biggest lever on both sale price and how fast something sells. This is worth doing right.

**Lighting.** Natural light near a window, during the day, is the default — soft and even. Avoid direct sun (harsh shadows, washed-out color). If you're shooting artificially, a softbox diffuses light better than a bare lamp.

**Background.** Plain white or gray, no pattern competing with the garment. A piece of white poster board works fine.

**Phone camera basics.** Turn off flash — it blows out highlights and distorts color. Turn on grid lines to keep the garment level. Tap to focus on the fabric, not the background. Keep white balance consistent so colors read true — "the item was a different color than pictured" is a top cause of returns.

**Shot list, in order:**
1. Hero shot — worn on-body if you can. On-model photos convert 20–30% higher than flat-lay and cut fit-related returns.
2. Back of the garment.
3. Brand/size tag, close up.
4. Fabric content tag, close up (buyers search by material).
5. Any flaw, stain, or repair — photographed honestly, in normal light, not cropped out.
6. Measurement shot — a tape measure laid across the garment, clearly readable.
7. Any distinguishing detail (buttons, embroidery, hardware, logo).

**Photo limits by platform:** Poshmark 16, eBay 24, Mercari ~12, Vinted ~20 (practical sweet spot 4–12), Depop just 4 — if you're short on slots, keep hero → tag → flat-lay → flaw and drop the rest.

**Common mistakes:** dark/blurry photos, cluttered backgrounds (a messy bed, patterned carpet), wrinkled or pet-hair-covered garments, inconsistent lighting across your listings. If you're photographing several items in one session, shoot them all under the same setup — it's faster and it makes your listings look consistent.

## 3. Choosing a platform

| Your item | Best platform | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Designer/luxury (Chanel, LV, Gucci) | The RealReal, Vestiaire Collective | Built-in authentication, higher-dollar buyers |
| Streetwear/menswear (Supreme, Rick Owens, vintage band tees) | Grailed | Buyers who know and pay for the niche |
| Everyday brands, women's fashion, activewear | Poshmark | Large, fashion-focused audience, willing to pay more |
| Fast fashion, high volume, low price point | Vinted | Zero seller fees — keep the whole sale price |
| Simple, beginner-friendly, mixed inventory | Mercari | Straightforward listing flow, no social-selling learning curve |
| Rare/collectible, or you want price discovery | eBay | Auction format, best sold-comps data of any platform |
| Bulk closet clean-out, minimal effort acceptable | ThredUp | Mail it in, they handle everything — lowest payout, least work |

**Fees, roughly (verify current numbers before relying on them — they change):**
- **Vinted**: 0% seller fee. Buyer pays a small protection fee at checkout instead.
- **Depop**: 0% commission, ~3.3% + $0.45 payment processing.
- **Mercari**: 10% flat on item + shipping.
- **Grailed**: ~9% commission + ~3.5% processing (~12.5% total).
- **eBay**: ~13.6–15.3% of the total transaction (including shipping), plus a small per-order fee.
- **Poshmark**: flat $2.95 under $15, 20% commission at $15+.
- **Vestiaire Collective**: tiered 12–25% by item value, plus 3% processing.
- **The RealReal / ThredUp**: full-service consignment — lowest seller take-home %, but they do the photography, listing, and selling for you.

## 4. Pricing

**Always price from sold listings, never active ones.** Active/asking prices show what people *hope* to get. Sold listings show what buyers actually paid. eBay's sold-listing filter has the deepest history and is worth checking even if you're selling somewhere else. Search brand + item + size, filter to sold, and look at the range — not just one number.

**Condition is the biggest price lever.** A small stain can cut the price by ~40% versus an otherwise-identical clean item. After condition, the next biggest factors are brand recognition, size (mid-range sizes sell faster than very small/large), and season (list coats in fall, not July).

**Two pricing approaches:**
- **Price low and let it move fast.** Works for most items, especially fast fashion and anything on Vinted/Mercari.
- **Price ~15% above your real target, then use offers to close the gap.** Works well on Poshmark specifically, where "offers to likers" (a private, time-limited discount you can send anyone who liked the listing) is a built-in closing tool.

**Set your floor before you list**, not in the moment — factor in the platform's fee %, and any shipping cost you're covering. Don't decline a lowball outright; counter it. Most successful negotiated sales start with a lowball that gets countered to a middle price.

**If it hasn't sold in ~2 weeks**, cut the price 10–15% and refresh the listing (re-list or "share/bump" it, depending on the platform) — this resets its position in feed-based search.

## 5. Writing the listing

**Title formula:** `Brand + Item Type + Distinguishing Style/Model + Key Attribute (color/material) + Size`

Example: *"Lululemon Align High Rise Leggings Black Size 6"*

Brand goes first because it's the single most-filtered search term on every platform. Size, color, and condition come next because buyers use them as hard filters. Save style keywords (Y2K, oversized, vintage) for the end, and only if they're actually accurate — several platforms detect and penalize keyword-stuffing.

**Description:** front-load the most important facts (brand, size, condition, key measurements) — buyers skim, they don't read top to bottom. One fact per line reads better than a dense paragraph. Always include measurements and condition in the text, even if they're also in separate listing fields.

**Measurements — flat, not on-body.** Lay the garment flat and smooth. For most tops, measure pit-to-pit (armpit seam to armpit seam) and double it for full chest circumference. For pants, measure the waistband straight across (doubled) and the inseam along the inner leg (not doubled). Photograph the tape measure laid across the garment — this one habit meaningfully cuts size-related disputes, since brand size tags are notoriously inconsistent.

## 6. Shipping

**Weigh everything, including packaging**, before buying or printing a label — label price tiers are unforgiving about rounding up.

**Poly mailers are the default for clothing.** Soft goods don't need rigid protection, mailers are cheaper (~$0.25 vs ~$1.25 for a comparable box), lighter, and water-resistant. Reserve boxes for structured or embellished pieces (beading, sequins) that could crease or catch in a mailer.

**Carrier:** USPS Ground Advantage is the standard choice for clothing — cheap, includes tracking and $100 of insurance by default. Most marketplaces (Poshmark, Mercari, Depop, Vinted) generate a prepaid label for you automatically once an item sells. If you're shipping outside one of those built-in systems (eBay, Grailed, or a sale that started elsewhere), **Pirate Ship** is a free label reseller offering commercial rates — often meaningfully cheaper than paying at the post office counter.

**Ship within 1–2 business days of a sale.** Slow handling time hurts your seller rating on every platform. Always ship with tracking, to the address the platform's order system gives you — not an address a buyer messages you directly, since that protects you if there's ever a dispute.

## 7. Common mistakes (and the fix)

1. **Bad photos** — biggest lever on both price and speed. Fix: natural light, plain background, full shot list.
2. **Pricing off active listings instead of sold ones** — leads to overpriced, stale listings. Fix: always filter to sold comps.
3. **Hiding flaws** — leads to returns and rating damage. Fix: photograph and disclose every real flaw, price accordingly.
4. **Listing too much too fast** — rushed photos and pricing on day one. Fix: work in small batches, same discipline every time.
5. **Slow shipping** — hurts your seller metrics everywhere. Fix: build 1–2 day handling into your routine, don't treat it as an afterthought.
6. **Declining lowballs instead of countering** — kills sales that could've closed. Fix: always counter, using the floor price you already set.
7. **Wrong platform for the item** — a $8 fast-fashion top on The RealReal, or a real designer piece on Vinted, both leave money or opportunity on the table. Fix: use the platform table in Section 3 before you list.
8. **Forgetting fees when pricing** — listing at your exact target take-home means you'll net less than expected once the platform takes its cut. Fix: back into your list price from your desired net.
9. **Not weighing the package before buying a label** — leads to overage charges or a shipment held for insufficient postage. Fix: weigh with all packaging included, every time.
